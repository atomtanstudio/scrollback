# AI Business / Distribution Playbook

The business opportunity is not "use AI to make more stuff." The useful pattern
is to package AI-enabled workflows into repeatable distribution, operations, and
service systems that create measurable outcomes: users, posts shipped, invoices
sent, revenue collected, or client assets delivered.

The strongest saved material points to three money paths:

1. Build a distribution engine around a product.
2. Sell AI-powered creative or operational services.
3. Use OpenClaw-style agents as a lightweight company operating system.

## Play 1: OpenClaw Distribution Engine

The Elijah Khan article is the most directly applicable business source. It
argues that before building harder, the founder should validate the problem,
interview users, build a waitlist, and keep the market warm before launch. It
then shows OpenClaw as the orchestration layer for a content/distribution system
that connects Notion and Postiz.

Core system:

- Notion is the content source of truth.
- OpenClaw validates rows marked ready to schedule.
- Postiz handles multi-platform scheduling.
- The sync writes Postiz IDs back to Notion and reconciles published status.
- A report goes to Discord.
- Duplicate prevention happens through Notion IDs, local tracking, and live
  Postiz checks.

This is valuable because distribution is an operational system, not a one-off
content idea. The repeatable asset is the workflow.

## Play 2: AI Creative Services

The Amir Mušić article is the strongest service-business play. Its frame is
simple: many people can generate assets; fewer people can package, sell, and
deliver deployment-ready work.

Service levels:

- **Level 1: Deployment-ready assets.** Sell GIFs, motion banners, deck openers,
  ad creatives, and formatted assets to brands that need fast production.
- **Level 2: Branding upsell.** Turn static brand deliverables into a working
  brand kit with motion assets and deployment-ready variants.
- **Level 3: Agency motion department.** Partner with branding agencies that do
  not offer motion, and handle motion under their name.

The durable lesson is that the sale is not "AI generation." The sale is a
pipeline that produces assets in the right format for a buyer with an urgent
distribution or sales need.

## Play 3: Agentic Service Loops

The clipping-agency thread provides a concrete autonomous-service pattern:

- Video clipper/distributor finds highlights, adds captions/effects, and
  distributes to TikTok/Reels/Shorts.
- Cold email outreach agent prospects creators, follows up, and books calls.
- Thumbnail/CTR agent tests thumbnails and optimizes per platform.
- A loop runs every 30 minutes: reason, act, observe, learn.

The proposed pricing ladder is simple: sell one-off clips for a small amount or
move creators to monthly retainers. The larger lesson is to productize a narrow
creator-business workflow where output and ROI are easy to understand.

## Play 4: Agent Company Operating System

The "AI company legally incorporated" article is useful because it describes
handoffs, not prompts. The durable company architecture is:

- A Chief of Staff agent receives most tasks.
- Specialist agents handle finance, operations, memory, and delivery.
- Handoffs include context, responsibility, and next action.
- The finance agent can generate invoices, log deals, remind before deadlines,
  reconcile payments, and update memory.

The key line of thinking: capable agents are not a company until handoffs exist.
Handoffs are the spine of the organization.

## What This Means For Our Work

The near-term business strategy should be biased toward workflows that:

- produce external-facing assets or distribution,
- can be run by OpenClaw agents repeatedly,
- create visible ROI for a founder, creator, or small business,
- generate case studies from our own use,
- and can later become a template, skill, or service package.

Best first experiments:

1. Build our own Notion/Postiz-style distribution pipeline.
2. Build a FeedSilo/GBrain research-to-content pipeline.
3. Productize an AI creative asset workflow for a narrow buyer.
4. Use Judy/OpenClaw as Chief of Staff for content, sales follow-up, and memory.

## Operating Rule

Do not save every business tweet to GBrain. Promote:

- validated workflows,
- pricing models,
- buyer profiles,
- repeatable prompts/skills,
- and evidence that a path produced revenue or distribution.

FeedSilo remains the archive. GBrain keeps the playbook.
